# Round-3 traits-delta memo — bestseller patterns vs. our rounds 1–2

Comparative deep-research pass (Cowork/Chrome, 2026-07-21). Etsy searched with
the Bestseller-badge filter across the shop's six niche families (mid-century
modern botanical, art deco geometric, minimalist landscape, wildflower
botanical, vintage herbarium, Japanese woodblock botanical) plus one adjacent
fauna-in-botanical sweep. Traits only — no competitor imagery saved, no shops
or listings named. Our rounds-1/2 side is taken from the round-3 plan §1
scorecard (candidates 15–24 owner grades), not from re-opening our images.

Every recorded trait ships with its **applicability condition** — the FM-1
lesson: a bare "bestsellers use circles" is what caused a prior failure mode.
"Backdrop devices appear behind X-scale subjects in Y compositions" is the
only acceptable form.

## Axis 1 — Secondary-subject integration (our weakest axis, FM-7)

**Bestseller pattern, with condition.** When a creature appears in a botanical
or scene listing it is almost never a small static motif dropped into a gap.
It takes one of two forms, each condition-bound:

- *When the creature is the point of the piece,* it is rendered **large and
  dominant** — a heron standing near full frame height with its legs grounded
  in the reeds/water; a single butterfly spanning well over half the frame
  width with **both wings spread symmetrically** and anatomically complete.
  Condition: applies to listings where fauna IS the focal subject, in a dense
  botanical or a flat-ground composition — the creature reads across a room.
- *When the creature is small, it appears in numbers and in motion* — several
  swallows mid-flight, wings open, sweeping diagonally across a flat colour
  field; a tiny human figure walking a forest path, feet on the path.
  Condition: small occupants are shown airborne/moving on a plain ground or
  physically travelling through the scene, never a lone still dot in negative
  space.

The through-line: **grounded occupants make physical contact** (legs in reeds,
feet on the path) and **airborne occupants need no perch but are clearly in
motion**. Anatomy is complete and symmetric.

**What our rounds 1–2 did instead.** Occupant present but mis-integrated:
dragonfly too small (15); "sun disc" shrunk to an odd small same-colour circle
(16); beetle superimposed on the stem with zero physical connection (22);
ladybug smudged and half-merged into a neighbouring flower (23); butterfly
placed awkwardly *below* the bloom and **missing a wing** (24); a seabird that
should have been flying or absent, perched and static (20).

**Brief-writing implication.** When an occupant is used, state its **relative
scale in positive terms** (large enough to read across a room / spanning a
good third-plus of the frame — err bigger), its **physical contact in verbs**
(legs gripping the stem, feet on the path) OR a **dynamic in-flight pose** if
it has no natural perch, and **anatomical completeness/symmetry** (both wings
spread evenly). All positive phrasing.

## Axis 2 — Backdrop / badge devices (the pendulum axis, FM-10)

**Bestseller pattern, with condition.** Backdrop devices are real and selling,
but **concentrated by composition type, not universal**:

- A plain **sun disc / circle** sits behind an **empty or atmospheric field** —
  a low sun over an otherwise empty sage sea; a pale sun inside a dense
  monochrome woodland. Condition: behind sparse or atmospheric scenes, never
  behind a busy floral.
- A **vertical striped band** sits behind a **single delicate stem**.
  Condition: one small botanical subject, generous surrounding calm.
- A **flat colour field** (e.g. mustard) sits behind **a few moderately-sized
  flying birds**. Condition: sparse moving subjects on an otherwise plain
  ground.
- Dense full-frame botanical / geometric / Matisse-cutout compositions use
  **no backdrop device at all** — the pattern fills the whole frame and IS the
  composition.

So the device lives on **small-subject, single-subject, or sparse-scene
listings**, and is absent on dense ones. A small red seal-stamp accent recurs
in Japanese pieces but functions as a signature accent, not a backdrop.

**What our rounds 1–2 did instead.** Round 1 put a backdrop shape in 8/10
(over-use); round 2 in 0/10 (extinction). Neither matched the selective
middle.

**Brief-writing implication.** Use a backdrop device in **1–2 of 5** briefs,
and **only on the sparse / single-subject briefs** (a single stem, one large
bloom, an empty-horizon landscape), sitting behind and touching the subject.
Never attach one to a dense full-frame brief.

## Axis 3 — Edge treatment (stem/botanical grounding, FM-9)

**Bestseller pattern, with condition.** Grounding is **idiom-dependent**:

- **Meadow / wildflower / bouquet** compositions root their stems at the
  **bottom edge** — stems rise from the lower frame, flowers at varied heights
  filling the width, no bottom margin. Condition: any multi-stem meadow or
  bouquet.
- **Single-specimen herbarium** deliberately **floats on cream with even
  margins on all four sides** — the margin is the point of the cut-specimen
  idiom, not a defect. Condition: one isolated botanical specimen presented as
  a plate.

The distinction that matters: a grounded meadow has **no** bottom margin; a
floating specimen has an **even** margin all round. An *uneven, one-sided
bottom band* is neither idiom — it is the defect.

**What our rounds 1–2 did instead.** Bouquet/herbarium compositions (16, 23)
stopped short of the bottom, leaving an unpleasant one-sided white band —
neither grounded nor evenly floated.

**Brief-writing implication.** For meadow/bouquet/stem subjects, state the
stems **run off / are rooted at the bottom edge**. For a deliberate specimen
float, state **even margins on all sides** — never leave grounding unstated,
which is when schnell reverts to a lopsided band.

## Axis 4 — Sparse vs. dense (owner's sparse ruling, FM-13)

**Bestseller pattern, with condition.** Sparse sells — **but only with a large
dominant subject or intentional atmosphere**:

- One **big** abstract flower filling the frame edge to edge; a large swirling
  tree; a big horse silhouette; a large butterfly — generous empty space
  around a subject that is unmistakably large and the star.
- Or the **emptiness itself is the subject** (an atmospheric empty sea/sky)
  finished with a clean device (the sun disc).

Condition: "one big subject + empty space" works when the subject is **large
and dominant**, or when the empty field is the intentional atmospheric
subject. A **small** subject stranded in a mostly empty frame does not appear
among bestsellers.

**What our rounds 1–2 did / owner ruling.** Aligns with the owner's §1 ruling:
big-subject-with-empty-space passes; tiny-subject-in-empty-frame fails. Round
2's critic wrongly rejected a legitimate large-sparse candidate.

**Brief-writing implication.** A sparse brief must state the dominant subject
is **large** (fills a majority of the frame / reads across a room). Never pair
"sparse / generous empty space" with a small subject.

## Axis 5 — Other divergences worth carrying

- **Palette clusters cleanly into our two families.** Warm earth (terracotta,
  burnt orange, mustard, olive, cream) dominates MCM/deco; muted cool
  naturalist (sage, teal, blue, deep green) dominates landscape/woodblock.
  Both bestseller lanes map onto our `saturated_retro` and `neutral` families
  — no need to invent a third. High-contrast bold-filled (black motifs on
  cream) also sells and reads as `neutral`.
- **Two boldness lanes both sell.** Bold filled graphic (MCM / deco / Matisse
  cutout) AND delicate painterly botanical (herbarium / wildflower) both carry
  badges. The delicate lane is harder for schnell's small-detail limit
  (cf. FM-12), so lean bold-filled for reliability while keeping one softer
  painterly brief in the batch for coverage.
- **Sets are common but each panel is one clean composition.** Bestsellers
  sell as sets of 2/3/6, but every panel is a single, self-contained,
  edge-managed composition — consistent with our one-listing-per-aspect-group
  model; nothing to change.
- **Typography features in some bestsellers** ("BAUHAUS", "VOLAR" over a
  butterfly) — out of scope for us (we don't render reliable text), noted only
  so it isn't mistaken for a trait to copy.
